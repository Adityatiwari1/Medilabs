What Is Borosilicate Glass?

Borosilicate glass is a type of glass that incorporates boric oxide (B₂O₃) as a network-forming oxide alongside the primary silica (SiO₂) network. The typical composition of borosilicate glass 3.3 — the international standard for laboratory glassware — is approximately: 81% SiO₂, 13% B₂O₃, 4% Na₂O/K₂O, and 2% Al₂O₃.

The addition of boric oxide disrupts the regular silica network structure in a way that dramatically reduces the coefficient of thermal expansion and improves chemical resistance. The "3.3" in "borosilicate 3.3" refers to the coefficient of linear thermal expansion: 3.3 × 10⁻⁶ per Kelvin (compared to approximately 9 × 10⁻⁶/K for soda-lime glass — nearly three times higher).

Historical Context: Invented for the Lab

Borosilicate glass was developed by German glassmaker Otto Schott in 1887, specifically to address the thermal shock problems experienced in laboratory and pharmaceutical glassware. Schott and Zeiss collaborated to develop the composition that became the foundation for modern lab glass. The trademarked "Pyrex" (Corning, 1915) brought borosilicate to wider recognition. Today, every reputable laboratory glassware manufacturer — including Medilab Exports Consortium — uses borosilicate 3.3 as the standard material.

Thermal Resistance — Withstanding Laboratory Extremes

The single most important practical advantage of borosilicate glass in laboratories is its ability to withstand rapid temperature changes without cracking — a property called thermal shock resistance.

Understanding Thermal Shock

When glass is heated or cooled rapidly, different parts of the glass expand or contract at different rates, creating internal stresses. If those stresses exceed the tensile strength of the glass, it fractures. With a CTE of 3.3 × 10⁻⁶/K, borosilicate glass expands approximately 2.7 times less than soda-lime glass for the same temperature change — meaning much lower internal stresses and dramatically better thermal shock resistance.

In practical terms: a borosilicate beaker can be placed on a hot plate and later rinsed with cool water without cracking. The same action with a soda-lime glass vessel would likely result in shattering — a serious safety hazard with hazardous chemicals involved.

Autoclaving and Sterilization

Borosilicate glassware can be autoclaved repeatedly at 121°C (250°F), 15 psi steam pressure — the standard sterilization cycle — without dimensional change or degradation. This is essential for microbiological and pharmaceutical laboratory applications. Soda-lime glass is unsuitable for repeated autoclaving due to accelerated chemical attack and dimensional instability.

Chemical Resistance in Laboratory Environments

Borosilicate glass 3.3 demonstrates exceptional resistance to a wide range of chemicals encountered in laboratory settings. This chemical inertness is critical to prevent two problems: glass degradation (etching, cloudiness, weakening) and sample contamination (glass leaching alkali metal ions into the sample).

What Borosilicate Glass Resists

  • Acids (except HF and hot concentrated phosphoric acid): Resists HCl, HNO₃, H₂SO₄, HClO₄, and most organic acids at all concentrations and temperatures typically used in labs
  • Most organic solvents: Resists acetone, ethanol, methanol, toluene, chloroform, dichloromethane, and most common lab solvents
  • Dilute alkali solutions: Resists dilute NaOH and KOH at room temperature; note that concentrated hot alkali can slowly etch borosilicate glass over time
  • Aqueous solutions and biological media: Fully resistant; minimal leaching even with prolonged contact

What Can Attack Borosilicate Glass

  • Hydrofluoric acid (HF): Attacks all silica-based glass immediately. PTFE or polyethylene must be used for HF work
  • Concentrated hot phosphoric acid: Can slowly attack borosilicate at elevated temperatures
  • Strong hot alkali (NaOH/KOH >2M at elevated temperatures): Causes gradual surface etching with long-term use
ISO Standard Fact: Borosilicate glass 3.3 is classified as Hydrolytic Class 1 (HGB 1) per ISO 719, meaning it releases less than 0.10 mL of 0.01 M HCl equivalent per gram of glass after a standardized hot-water leaching test. This is the highest chemical resistance rating for commercial glass — essential for pharmaceutical and trace analysis applications.

Borosilicate vs Soda-Lime Glass — A Clear Comparison

Understanding the differences between borosilicate and soda-lime glass is essential for laboratory procurement decisions. While soda-lime glass is cheaper to produce (it melts at lower temperatures and requires less expensive raw materials), its performance limitations make it unsuitable for most laboratory applications.

Soda-lime glass (also called "soft glass") has poor thermal shock resistance, higher ion leaching (classified as Hydrolytic Class 3), and lower maximum service temperature. Its use in labs is limited to: basic disposable items (certain culture tubes, some reagent bottles where Type II performance is acceptable), applications where the glass does not contact the sample directly, and educational demonstrations where chemical inertness is not critical.

For all precision lab work, analytical chemistry, pharmaceutical applications, and any use involving heat, hazardous chemicals, or sensitive samples — borosilicate glass 3.3 is the only appropriate choice.

Is Borosilicate Glass Safe for Pharmaceutical Laboratories?

Borosilicate glass 3.3 is the glass of choice for pharmaceutical applications and is classified as "Type I" glass under the United States Pharmacopeia (USP), European Pharmacopoeia (EP), and other major pharmacopoeias. This classification — the highest for pharmaceutical glass containers — confirms that it meets the most stringent requirements for chemical resistance and low leachability.

In pharmaceutical manufacturing and QC laboratories, Type I borosilicate glass is used for: primary packaging (ampoules, vials) of injectable drug products; laboratory vessels for drug analysis and QC testing; standard solution preparation and titration; and dissolution testing equipment. Its use in pharmaceutical settings is backed by decades of regulatory precedent worldwide.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between borosilicate 3.3 and borosilicate 5.1?

The numbers refer to the coefficient of linear thermal expansion: borosilicate 3.3 has a CTE of 3.3 × 10⁻⁶/K and borosilicate 5.1 has 5.1 × 10⁻⁶/K. Borosilicate 3.3 (also called "hard glass" or Type I) is the international standard for laboratory glassware — it has lower thermal expansion, better thermal shock resistance, and higher chemical resistance. Borosilicate 5.1 (sometimes called "intermediate" or "low expansion" glass) is occasionally used in some industrial applications but is not the standard for precision laboratory glassware. All Medilab Exports laboratory glassware uses borosilicate 3.3.

Can borosilicate laboratory glassware be autoclaved?

Yes — borosilicate glass can be autoclaved repeatedly at standard sterilization conditions (121°C, 15 psi, 15–20 minutes) without dimensional change, cracking, or significant chemical degradation. This makes it ideal for microbiological and pharmaceutical laboratory applications where sterility is required. Important: allow glassware to cool slowly after autoclaving — do not place hot autoclaved glassware on cold surfaces (thermal shock risk). Volumetric glassware should be recalibrated if autoclaved repeatedly, as sustained heat exposure can theoretically cause very minor dimensional changes over many cycles.

Does borosilicate glass react with any chemicals?

Borosilicate glass is attacked by hydrofluoric acid (HF) and concentrated hot phosphoric acid — avoid using borosilicate containers for HF work (use PTFE or polyethylene instead). Hot concentrated alkali solutions (NaOH, KOH above 2M at elevated temperatures) cause gradual surface etching with prolonged contact. For all other common laboratory chemicals — acids, organic solvents, aqueous solutions, biological media — borosilicate 3.3 is highly resistant and safe to use.

How is borosilicate glass different from Pyrex?

Pyrex is a brand name (originally by Corning, now licensed to different manufacturers in different regions) for a borosilicate glass product. The original Pyrex glass was borosilicate with a CTE of approximately 3.3 × 10⁻⁶/K. However, in some markets (notably North America), "Pyrex" branded consumer cookware has been reformulated to soda-lime glass — so "Pyrex" no longer reliably means borosilicate. For laboratory use, always specify "borosilicate glass 3.3" or "Type I borosilicate glass" rather than relying on brand names.
